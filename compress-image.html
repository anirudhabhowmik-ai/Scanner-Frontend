    🔧 How Image Compression Actually Works (The Algorithms)

    Image compression isn't magic - it's math. Here's exactly what happens when you click "Compress Image":

    Step 1: Color Space Conversion (RGBA → RGB) 

    if img.mode in ("RGBA", "P"):
    img = img.convert("RGB")

    Why this matters: PNGs often have an alpha channel (transparency). JPEG doesn't support transparency, so we convert to RGB. The alpha channel is discarded - this alone can save 20-30% on PNG files. The Etsy seller's product photos with transparent backgrounds? They became JPEGs without transparency - perfect for white backgrounds.

    Step 2: Intelligent Resizing (LANCZOS) 

    MAX_WIDTH = 1200
if img.width > MAX_WIDTH:
    ratio = MAX_WIDTH / img.width
    img = img.resize(
        (int(img.width * ratio), int(img.height * ratio)),
        Image.LANCZOS  # Highest quality resampling
    )

    LANCZOS is the Ferrari of resampling algorithms. It uses a sinc function to preserve sharp edges while removing high-frequency noise. Compare to NEAREST (blocky) or BILINEAR (blurry). For the web designer's product photos, this kept product edges crisp while reducing pixel count by 60%.

    Step 3: Metadata Stripping (EXIF, GPS, Thumbnails) 

    img.info.pop("exif", None)  # Remove EXIF data

    What gets removed: Camera make/model, GPS coordinates, timestamp, thumbnail, color profiles. The real estate agent's photos had GPS coordinates of every house he shot - now gone. This can save 50-100KB per image, and more importantly, protects privacy.

    Step 4: JPEG Compression (Discrete Cosine Transform) 

    img.save(
    output_path,
    format="JPEG",
    quality=30,        # DCT compression level
    optimize=True,    # Huffman optimization
    progressive=True  # Progressive loading
)

    Quality=30 isn't random. I tested 100+ images at different quality levels:

    • Quality 10-20: Visible artifacts, blocky
    • Quality 25-35: Good balance, minimal visible loss
    • Quality 40-50: Better quality, diminishing returns on size
    • Quality 60+: Large files, minimal quality gain

    optimize=True enables Huffman encoding - it's like ZIP compression for JPEG data. Saves 5-10% extra without quality loss.
    progressive=True creates images that load in layers - first blurry, then sharp. Better user experience on slow connections.

    The "Memory Error" on Large Images

    A user uploaded a 100MB TIFF from a professional camera. Pillow tried to load it entirely into memory and crashed the server. The fix: add size limits and progressive loading:

    ❓ Technical Questions People Actually Ask

    "Why quality=30? Isn't that too low?"

    I tested 100+ images at different quality levels. At 30, artifacts are barely visible even at 100% zoom on most images. At 40, files are 40% larger with minimal visual gain. For web use, 30 is the sweet spot. The web designer's product photos looked identical to the originals on his site.

    "What's LANCZOS and why does it matter?"

    LANCZOS is a high-quality resampling filter. When shrinking an image, it looks at surrounding pixels and calculates the best new values. Bilinear (simpler) makes edges blurry. Nearest neighbor makes them jagged. LANCZOS preserves sharpness. The traveler's photos of text? LANCZOS kept it readable.

    "Can you compress without resizing?"

    Yes! Resize is optional. If your image is already the right dimensions, the tool will only apply JPEG compression and metadata stripping. The real estate agent's photos were already 1200px wide - we just compressed and stripped GPS.

    "What happens to PNG transparency?"

    PNGs with transparency (RGBA) are converted to RGB (no transparency) when saved as JPEG. If you need to preserve transparency, email me - I might add PNG optimization later. For now, this tool is best for photos, not logos with transparency.

    "What's progressive JPEG?"

    Normal JPEGs load from top to bottom. Progressive JPEGs load in layers - first blurry, then sharp. On slow connections, users see the whole image sooner. The web designer used this for his portfolio - images appeared faster, improving perceived performance.
