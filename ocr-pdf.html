    What is OCR? (And Why You Might Need It)

    OCR stands for Optical Character Recognition. Fancy name, simple idea: it's software that reads text from images.

    Here's the problem: when you scan a document or take a photo of a page, you end up with an image file. Your computer sees a picture, not words. You can't search for "rent increase" in a scanned lease agreement because to the computer, it's just a photo of words, not actual text.

    OCR fixes that. It looks at the image, figures out which letters and numbers are where, and adds a hidden text layer underneath. The document looks identical, but now you can search, copy, and highlight text. It turns your scanner into something actually useful.

    How It Actually Works (No Tech Jargon)

    Step 1: Clean up the image. The software adjusts contrast, removes noise, and straightens things out so it can "read" better.

    Step 2: Find the text. It looks for blocks of text, columns, paragraphs - anything that looks like words.

    Step 3: Recognize each letter. It compares what it sees against patterns it knows. That shape could be an "a" or an "o" - context helps figure it out.

    Step 4: Add the text layer. It creates an invisible layer of actual text that sits on top of your document. You still see the original scan, but now you can interact with the words.

    Tips From Someone Who's Processed Thousands of Pages

    300 DPI is the Sweet Spot

    If you're scanning, set your scanner to 300 DPI (dots per inch). Lower than that and text gets blurry. Higher than that and files get huge without improving accuracy much. 300 is the Goldilocks setting.

    Straighten Before Scanning

    A page scanned at a 10-degree angle can still be read, but OCR accuracy drops. Take the extra second to line it up straight on the scanner. For photos of documents, try to shoot straight-on rather than from an angle.

    Clean Originals Matter

    Coffee stains, highlighter marks, crumpled pages, faded print - all of these confuse OCR. If the document is important and the original is rough, consider finding a cleaner copy or at least smoothing out wrinkles before scanning.

    Pick Your Language

    If your document is in Spanish, tell the tool it's Spanish. If it mixes English and French, select both. The OCR engine uses language patterns to guess words correctly. Selecting the right language boosts accuracy noticeably.

    Proofread Important Stuff

    OCR is good, not perfect. For legal documents or anything where accuracy matters, spot-check the results. Names, numbers, and dates are most likely to have errors. I always double-check those.
