Which Image Format Should You Pick?

üì∏ PNG - For Perfect Quality

The baker uses PNG for her cake photos - every detail matters. PNG is lossless, which means no quality loss at all. Text stays sharp, colors stay accurate, images look exactly like your PDF.
Best for: Menus, flyers, documents with text, anything where quality matters more than file size.
Downside: Files can be big. A full page at 300 DPI might be 5-10MB.

üñºÔ∏è JPG - For Smaller Files

The social media manager uses JPG for Instagram posts. JPG shrinks files by removing some detail you probably won't notice. A 10MB PNG might become a 1MB JPG that looks almost the same on a phone screen.
Best for: Social media, email attachments, websites, anywhere file size matters.
Downside: Every time you save a JPG, it loses a tiny bit of quality. Too much compression and you'll see blocky artifacts.

üåê WEBP - Modern and Efficient

The artist uses WEBP for her website portfolio. WEBP is Google's format that combines PNG quality with JPG file sizes. Same quality as PNG, 25-35% smaller.
Best for: Websites, modern apps, anywhere you want great quality AND small files.
Downside: Some older software can't open it. But everything modern does.

What is DPI? (And Why Should You Care?)

DPI stands for Dots Per Inch. Think of it as how many tiny dots of color are packed into every inch of your image. More dots = sharper image = bigger file.

üì± 72 DPI - For Screens

Computer screens and phones can't show more than about 72-96 DPI anyway. Using higher DPI for web images just makes files bigger without looking better. The social media manager uses 72 DPI for all her posts.
Perfect for: Instagram, Facebook, websites, emails, anything viewed on screens.

üñ®Ô∏è 300 DPI - For Printing

This is the magic number for print. Magazines, brochures, flyers - they all expect 300 DPI. The artist sends galleries 300 DPI images of her work. Lower than that, and prints look fuzzy.
Perfect for: Flyers, business cards, professional prints, anything going on paper.

üé® 600 DPI - For Special Cases

Only need this for huge prints (posters, banners) or when you might need to zoom way in later. The event planner uses 600 DPI for wedding programs that get printed large.
Perfect for: Large posters, fine art prints, archival copies.

‚ö†Ô∏è Warning: File size explodes as DPI goes up. A full page at 72 DPI might be 200KB. Same page at 600 DPI could be 10MB. Choose wisely.

Stuff That Goes Wrong (And How to Fix It)

üñºÔ∏è "The images are blurry"

Increase DPI. 72 DPI is for screens, but if you're printing or need detail, go to 300. Also check quality setting - if it's below 70, JPG compression might be too aggressive.

üì¶ "The file is huge"

Lower the DPI (72 is fine for web). Lower quality (85 instead of 100). Switch from PNG to JPG or WEBP. The teacher had 50MB slides - dropped to 3MB with JPG at 85 quality.

üé® "The colors look wrong"

PDF and images use different color systems. For color-critical work, use PNG at maximum quality. The artist checks her portfolio images on multiple screens before sending.

üîç "Text is hard to read"

Use PNG, not JPG. JPG smears text. PNG preserves every sharp edge. The teacher uses PNG for all text-heavy slides - students can read every word.

üîí "It won't upload my PDF"

Password protected? Use the Unlock PDF tool first. Files over 50MB might time out - try splitting into smaller PDFs first.
