🔧 How PDF to Image Conversion Actually Works

When you upload a PDF, here's what happens behind the scenes:

Step 1: Parse Page Range 

# Convert user-friendly range to zero-indexed pages
if page_range == "all":
    pages_to_convert = list(range(total_pages))
elif "-" in page_range:  # "1-5"
    start, end = page_range.split("-")
    pages_to_convert = list(range(int(start)-1, int(end)))
elif "," in page_range:  # "1,3,5"
    pages_to_convert = [int(p)-1 for p in page_range.split(",")]

The event planner only needs pages 2, 4, and 6 for the website. The parser converts "2,4,6" to zero-indexed [1,3,5] and only renders those pages.

Step 2: DPI and Zoom Factor 

# Base DPI in PDFs is 72
zoom = dpi / 72
matrix = fitz.Matrix(zoom, zoom)

# Example: 300 DPI → zoom = 4.17x
# A letter-size page (8.5×11") becomes 2550×3300 pixels

The teacher uses 300 DPI for printable slides - the math works out perfectly: 8.5" × 300 DPI = 2550 pixels wide.

Step 3: Rendering with PyMuPDF 

pix = page.get_pixmap(matrix=matrix, alpha=False)
img_data = pix.tobytes("png")

get_pixmap() renders the page as a bitmap. alpha=False means no transparency - we'll handle that in Pillow if needed. The artist's portfolio with white backgrounds renders perfectly.

Step 4: RGBA to RGB Conversion (for JPG) 

if image_format == "jpg" and img.mode == "RGBA":
    rgb_img = Image.new("RGB", img.size, (255, 255, 255))
    rgb_img.paste(img, mask=img.split()[3])  # Use alpha as mask
    img = rgb_img

JPG doesn't support transparency. This code composites transparent areas against white - perfect for menus and documents. The social media manager's images have white backgrounds, exactly what Instagram expects.

Step 5: Quality and Compression 

if image_format == "png":
    compress_level = int((100 - quality) / 11)  # quality 95 → level 0 (no compression)
    img.save(img_io, format="PNG", optimize=True, compress_level=compress_level)
elif image_format == "jpg":
    img.save(img_io, format="JPEG", quality=quality, optimize=True)

PNG uses compress_level (0-9) where 0 is no compression. JPG uses quality (1-100) where 100 is best quality, largest file. The teacher's slides at quality 95 are perfect for printing, files are manageable.

📐 DPI Deep Dive - The Math Behind Resolution

DPI (Dots Per Inch) determines how many pixels your image will have. Here's the exact math: 

Image width (pixels) = page width (inches) × DPI
Image height (pixels) = page height (inches) × DPI

# Standard page sizes in inches:
Letter: 8.5 × 11 inches

🖼️ Image Format Comparison - When to Use Each

PNG (Lossless) 

# PNG compression: DEFLATE algorithm
- Compression ratio: 2:1 to 5:1
- Quality: 100% lossless
- Best for: Text, diagrams, screenshots
- Transparency: Yes (RGBA)

The baker uses PNG for her menu - every detail of her cake photos matters. Text stays sharp, colors accurate.

JPG (Lossy)

# JPG compression: DCT (Discrete Cosine Transform)
- Compression ratio: 10:1 to 20:1
- Quality: Adjustable (1-100)
- Best for: Photos, social media
- Transparency: No

The social media manager uses JPG at quality 90 - files are 90% smaller than PNG, and nobody notices the difference on phones.

WEBP (Modern) 

# WEBP compression: VP8/VP9 video codec
- Compression ratio: 25-35% better than JPG
- Quality: Adjustable (1-100)
- Best for: Websites
- Transparency: Yes (lossless or lossy)

The artist uses WEBP for her website - same quality as PNG, 30% smaller files. Pages load faster.

🐛 The Debugging Stories (What Went Wrong)

The "Black Background" Mystery

Early users converting PDFs to JPG got images with black backgrounds where transparency should be. The issue: PDFs with alpha channel rendered as RGBA, and JPG can't handle alpha. 

# Fixed: Composite against white background
rgb_img = Image.new("RGB", img.size, (255, 255, 255))
rgb_img.paste(img, mask=img.split()[3] if img.mode == "RGBA" else None)

The "Page 0" Off-by-One

Users typing "1" expected page 1. But PyMuPDF uses zero-indexed pages. The parser was converting "1" to page 0 (correct), but error messages showed the wrong numbers.

📊 Quality vs Compression - The Numbers

Why quality 95 is the sweet spot for JPG:

📈 Why 95 Works

JPG's DCT algorithm at quality 95 removes high-frequency data that human eyes can barely perceive. A 10MB PNG becomes a 1.5MB JPG at quality 95, and most people can't tell the difference. The teacher's slides at quality 95 look perfect to students.

❓ Technical Questions People Actually Ask

"How does DPI affect image size?" 

# Pixel dimensions scale linearly with DPI
width_pixels = width_inches * DPI
height_pixels = height_inches * DPI
file_size ∝ width_pixels * height_pixels

Double the DPI = 4x the pixels (width × height) = roughly 4x the file size. The teacher's 150 DPI slides are 4x smaller than 300 DPI versions.

"Why does PNG compression use compress_level instead of quality?"

# quality 50 → compress_level 4 (slower, smaller)
# quality 1 → compress_level 9 (slowest, smallest)
