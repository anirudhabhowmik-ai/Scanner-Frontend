    What is PDF/A? (And Why Should You Care?)

    Think of PDF/A as the time capsule version of PDF. Regular PDFs might look fine today, but open them in 20 years and things can break. Fonts might be missing. Images might not load. Links might point to websites that don't exist anymore.

    üì¶ What Makes PDF/A Different

    • Everything is included: All fonts are embedded right in the file. Even if you don't have that font installed 50 years from now, the document still looks exactly right.
    • No outside dependencies: No links to external websites, no embedded videos, no dependencies on things that might disappear.
    • No encryption: Can't be password-locked, so future archivists can always open it. (Keep sensitive files in a secure archive instead.)
    • Self-contained: Everything needed to display the document is inside the file itself.

    How OCR Actually Works (No Tech Jargon)

    When you scan a document, you're really just taking a photo of each page. Your computer sees it as a picture, not as words. You can't search for "rent increase" in a scanned lease because to the computer, it's just a picture of a page.

    üëÅÔ∏è Step 1: Clean Up the Image

    First, we make the image as clear as possible. Remove smudges, adjust contrast, straighten pages that were scanned crooked. The cleaner the image, the better the text recognition.

    üî§ Step 2: Find the Text

    The system looks for patterns that look like letters and words. It doesn't just look at individual characters - it understands context. If it sees "c" and "at" next to each other, it knows that's probably "cat".

    üåç Step 3: Apply Language Rules

    This is where the 14+ languages come in. If you tell us the document is in Hindi, we use Hindi language patterns. If it's in English and Spanish, we use both. The translator who emailed me? His documents mixed English and Hindi. Selecting both languages made it work perfectly.

    üìÑ Step 4: Add the Text Layer

    We add an invisible text layer underneath your scanned image. The document still looks exactly like your original scan, but now you can search, copy, and highlight text.     Tips From Someone Who's Processed Thousands of Scanned Pages

    üìè Scan at 300 DPI Minimum

    Lower resolution = blurry text = bad OCR. 300 DPI is the sweet spot. Good enough for accurate text recognition, not so huge that files become unmanageable. For small text or old documents with faded ink, go to 400-600 DPI.

    üìÑ Keep Documents Flat and Straight

    Curved pages from books, wrinkled paper, or crooked scans confuse OCR. Flatten what you can, straighten pages before scanning. The librarian uses a book scanner for old newspapers - keeps pages flat, OCR works perfectly.

    üåç Select All Languages Present

    If your document mixes English and Hindi, select both. If it's mostly English but has Spanish names, select both. The OCR engine uses language patterns to guess words correctly. The translator's documents worked because he selected both languages.

    üîç Test Searchability Immediately

    After downloading, open the file and hit Ctrl+F (or Cmd+F on Mac). Search for a word you know is in there. If it finds it, you're good. If not, something went wrong. The PhD student tests every batch before deleting her original scans.

    üíæ Keep Originals

    Always keep the original scanned PDFs somewhere. OCR is good, not perfect. If you find a mistake later, you might need to reprocess. My uncle keeps an "originals" folder for every client, just in case.
