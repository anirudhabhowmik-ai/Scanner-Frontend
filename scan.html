Tips From Someone Who Scans Way Too Much

I've scanned hundreds of documents with this tool. Here's what I learned:

Lighting Matters (But You Don't Need Professional Lights)

Near a window during the day works great. At night, just turn on a lamp. The biggest issue is shadows - if your hand or phone casts a shadow on the document, move around until it's gone. I scan receipts on my kitchen counter under the overhead light and they come out fine.

The Background Trick

Put your document on something that's NOT the same color. White paper on a dark table? Perfect. White paper on a white table? The edge detection gets confused. I use a dark desk mat for receipts, or just grab whatever contrasting surface is around.

Don't Stand Too Close

You want some margin around the document. If you zoom in too tight, the edge detection might cut something off. I back up until there's maybe an inch of background showing on all sides. The tool crops it out anyway.

For OCR, Keep It Simple

If you need to extract text, make sure the document is flat and well-lit. Curved pages (like from a book) can distort text. Black text on white paper works best. Colored paper or fancy fonts make it harder for the OCR to read. I learned this the hard way trying to scan a receipt printed on thermal paper that was already fading.

Glossy Documents Are Annoying (Here's the Fix)

Shiny paper, laminated cards, or glossy photos reflect light and create glare. Tilt the document slightly or move your light source. Sometimes I scan at a slight angle on purpose, then let the perspective correction straighten it out - this eliminates the glare.

Stuff That Goes Wrong (And How to Fix It)

üì± Camera Won't Open

Your browser needs permission to use the camera. On iPhone/iPad, Safari will ask - tap "Allow." If you accidentally said no, check your browser settings or device settings. On Android, same deal - look for camera permissions. Also, make sure you're on HTTPS (you are, this site uses it). Some browsers are picky about that.

üìÑ Edge Detection Misses the Document

Usually means the background is too similar to the document. White paper on white table? The tool gets confused. Put something dark underneath - a jacket, a book cover, anything contrasting. If it still misses, you can drag the corners manually to fix it.

üì∏ Scan Is Blurry

Two common causes: camera shake or bad focus. Rest your phone on something if you can. Tap the screen to focus on the document. If it's still blurry, maybe the lighting is too dim - phones use slower shutter speeds in low light, which blur easily. Add more light.

üî§ OCR Text Is Garbage

OCR works best on clean, printed text. If you're getting nonsense, check: Is the text actually readable in the photo? Is the document flat (curved pages distort text)? Is the font really fancy? Handwriting? OCR hates handwriting. Try rescanning with better lighting and a flat document.

üì• Can't Download on iPhone

iOS handles downloads differently. If tapping download doesn't work, try: tap and hold the image, choose "Save Image." For PDFs, they might open in a new tab - look for the share icon (box with arrow) and choose "Save to Files." I know it's annoying, blame Apple, not me.

‚è≥ Processing Takes Forever

Really big images take longer to process. If your phone takes 48-megapixel photos (some new ones do), that's a lot of data. Try moving a bit farther away so the document is smaller in frame, or use the "upload image" option with a slightly lower resolution version.

"The OCR is giving me weird text. Why?"

OCR (text recognition) works best on clean, printed text. If your original is blurry, handwritten, or has weird fonts, the results won't be perfect. Try rescanning with better lighting and make sure the document is flat. If it's still bad, the original might just be too low quality.

"Can I scan a book?"

You can scan pages one by one. For a whole book, that's a lot of work. But for a few pages from a textbook? Works fine. Just watch out for curved pages near the spine - flatten them as best you can for better results.

"What's the maximum file size?"

No hard limit, but really huge images (like 50MB+) might take a while to process. Most phone photos are 2-5MB, which processes in seconds. If you're uploading giant scans from a professional camera, they'll work but be patient.
